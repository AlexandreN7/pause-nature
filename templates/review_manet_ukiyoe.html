Manet and Ukiyo-e

Manet and Ukiyo-e

Date : 10/02/2020 by Alexandre Proux under REVIEW

The Ukiyo-e artistic movement arrived in France in 1856 during the Impressionist period with the publication of Hokusai manga (sketches) and Hiroshige prints. In this article we analyze the influences of the Ukiyo-e artistic movement on Edouard Manet's paintings.


Ukiyo-e can be translated as the study of the living world. It illustrates the beauty of the natural and animal world. It gives a very special place to natural elements such as waves, wind or rain. Often, characters are relayed in the background of the painting and seem very small next to mother nature. The first collection of Japanese prints was created by Edmond de Goncourt who published biographies and leaflets of prints.
Edouard Manet was born in Paris on January 23, 1832 and he is one of the best known painters in the Impressionist art movement. Manet discovered the Japanese prints thanks to the engraver Eugène Lavieille.
The painting of the portrait of Emile Zola (above) illustrates very well the influence of Hokusai. In the background of the painting, we can see a folding screen and a woodcut of the Japanese artist Kuniaki II. On the table, there is a porcelain piece whose bluish patterns are probably inspired by japanese art.
Others evidences of the influence of the artistic movement ukiyo-e can be found in Manet's animals drawings. For instance, the drawing of a crow he made for the cover of Edgar Allan Poe's book leaves no doubt. His shape and lines seem directly extracted from Hokusai's mangas.


Do you want more evidence ? In his portait of Nina de Callais, the background of the painting is made up of fans whose illustrations recall the prints of Japanese masters. If you look closely, you can also distinguish a crane in the middle of the fans.

In Zacharie Astruc's painting (below), Manet painted a booklet of prints on the table next to the character.

To cut a long story short, if we look a little further Manet's works we can find more evidences of the influence of the ukiyoe movement, especially in his drawings of cats and flowers. To conclude, we can appreciate the work of Manet for being one of the first impressionists to mix western painting codes with references to Japanese prints balanced by his genius.
