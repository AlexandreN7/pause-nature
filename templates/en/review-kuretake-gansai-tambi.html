Review : Gansai Tambi Watercolor

Review : Gansai Tambi Watercolor

Date : 14/01/2020 by Alexandre Proux under REVIEW

The watercolor Gansai Tambi manufactured by the Japanese compagny Kuretake is now exported to european art supplies stores. Are there any differences between Japanese and Western watercolor? Coincidentally, I have 2 palettes of Gansai Tambi. Let's review it!

First impressions

Kuretake Gansai Tambi 12-color set was my first watercolor palette, back then I had absolutely no knowledge on watercolor and I decided to buy this palette probably because of its packaging. It was not even for the price, which at the time was higher than today (at least in Paris). I used it a lot and even decided to buy the 36-color set. Little tips, you can find these palettes for cheap on chinese website ( shhhhhh ....).

First thing first, I did a color chart of my 36-color set.

From the first brushstroke, it appears that the behavior of this watercolor is very different from western brands. The paint activates very easily from the first brush stoke with relatively little water. The large size of the pans makes lifting the paint easier, especially when using a large paintbrush. We can assume that this behavior is due to the binder that is based on a mixture of glue and glycerine synthetize from animal bones and skins.

First observations :

  • Good water activation
  • Some colors seem better than others. At first glance, red and green are intense and bright
  • Some colors seem not very pigmented, in particular : 61 (ultramarine pale) ,64 (ultramarine), 46 (burnt sienna) and 47(raw umber deep)
  • Paint is opaque and glossy / bright aspect when dry
  • No granulation
  • Reactivate easily
  • Behaves differently in comparison to traditional watercolor
  • AP (approved product) Seal (non toxic)


The most annoying thing about this painting is that the composition of this watercolor is a mystery. There no information on pigment used or binder on the official website. However, Kuretake announces that the painting was done with a traditional receipt. Well, we can try to believe it.

On internet, we can read that this watercolor is not suited for mixing and it is adviced to use it directly from the pan. This behavior could be due to the number of pigments constituting the color. Indeed, mixing 2 colors each constituted of several pigments tends to lead to a muddy color. A watercolor paint that you can't mix ? This may be heresy for some watercolorists.

Conclusion

Chromatography method shows quickly if a paint is cheap or not, and here, the results are far better from what I expected. The paint spreads evenly across the paper strips. We don't see a clear color separation, which is very positive. I think the colors I tried are mostly mono pigmented (I still have doubt on 30).

Despite these good results. I remain mixed on the use of this watercolor. The fact that the manufacturer does not indicate the pigments composing his paint leads to taking the risk of painting with pigments which would not be durable. A good number of pigments used in watercolor are not resistant to light / moisture, and their colors may change over time. In addition, at the sight of its price, I therefore advise against the use of this paint for any serious work. And I would only use it in the upstream work and the sketches.
