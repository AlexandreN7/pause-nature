Brief history of fountain pens and why they are relevant in watercolor

Transportable, durable and allowing the addition of contrast and personality to a drawing, the fountain pen is a tool of choice for the watercolourist. In this article, I quickly introduce how to choose a fountain pen for watercolor painting and sketching.


In the  olden time there was less choice than today. The word pen refers to the Latin penna, which means a feather or plume and describes to a feather pen. These pens made from animal feathers were used until the mid-19th century to make room for metallic pen. But for metal pens, there is no end. The first attemps at mettalic nib date back to the 18th century in England. Today the feather holders have given way to the fountain pen which, let's face it, is starting to disappear in favor of rollerball pens or technical pens.

Pen holders are still used marginally by refractory artists such as the mangaka. An this topic deserves a full article. Using a pen holder for drawing has a huge drawback, the need to have a bottle of ink handy and I let you imagine the discomfort for painting outdoors. And now I come to the subject of fountain pens. Fountain pens come in a huge variety of nib and body styles. Some pens are luxury items or even piece of art while others are very inexpensive.

First, to effectively use a fountain pen in watercolor painting, we need a waterproof ink bottle compatible with fountain pens. I wrote a little article on this topic : Black ink for watercolor . Personally, I chose the Platinum carbon ink, which is perfectly waterproof when dry. To put ink in the pen we need a piston converter or a fountain pen with a piston. Now comes the choice of the pen itself. There are many brands of fountain pens and a wide variety of choices. For the drawing, the determining parameters are concentrated on the nib.

Gold nib vs steel nib

We sometimes hear that gold nib are more flexible and with more rebound than steel nib. For me it is a purely commercial argument. My personal opinion is that the softness and rebound of a nib is the direct consequence of the shape and construction.

The only thing that matter, is that ink has corrosive properties and that the gold nibs are less sensivitive than steel nib. But contemporary steel nibs seem much more resistant than in the past.

Nib size ?

Fountain pen nibs come in many sizes. As we will use this pen for drawing, I recommended taking an extra fine size. Japanese nibs are thinner than western pen (Western extra fine = Japanese fine).

Flexible nib ?

Rather rare, some nibs have incrased flexibility. They are mainly used in calligraphy techniques, they offer very interesting line variation which can produce nive effects in watercolor painting.

Review : lamy 2000 fountain pen

My current choice is the Lamy 2000 with a fine nib.

  • Gold nib
  • Piston filling
  • Not very flexible
  • Around 200€
  • Weight ~25g

I bought this fountain pen after reading very positive reviews. On internet this pen is legendary. My personnal opinion is that it is a very solid construction and it seems very durable over time.

Let's be honest, my overall feeling is that this pen is maybe a bit overated and I find it a bit scratchy (at least with Lamy blue ink and normal paper). Moreover, the square shape of the point of the nib create a soft point which require a bit of training to reach. Fortunately, Platinum ink contains lubricants that solve the scratching problem. An interesting effect, the reverse writing of the pen draws very fine lines very appreciated for drawing details.

Conclusion

It is a good fountain pen, for drawing or daily writing. It's robust design makes it compatible with intesive use, even outdoors. It does its job, but eventually I will change to a Japanese fountain pen with a thinner tip and maybe more flexibility.
